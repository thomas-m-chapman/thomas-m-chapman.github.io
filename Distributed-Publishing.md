# Distributed Publishing: A Knowledge Management Case Study

---

## Executive Summary

BECU's Knowledge Management (KM) team was being overwhelmed by publishing requests. The backlog of requests had grown to approximately 300, the average time-to-publish (TTP) had climbed to two months, compliance escalations were becoming a recurring problem, and SME satisfaction was in decline.

To address these issues, I designed and led an initiative called Distributed Publishing. The objective was to enable a select group of manager-approved, KM-trained subject matter experts — called Advanced SMEs — to publish content directly to the knowledge base without KM intervention. 

The results were immediate and measurable: The backlog diminished from 300 to ~25, the average TTP dropped from 60 days to 7, compliance escalations dropped to 0, and SME satisfaction climbed from 1.9 to 4.2 (on a 5-point scale).

The most significant impact was cultural. Distributed Publishing gave SMEs a stronger sense of independence and trust, and increased their sense of ownership, motivation, and engagement in the knowledge-sharing mission. Distributed Publishing continues to be an integral component of KM at BECU today.

---

## Situation

When I joined BECU as Senior Manager of Knowledge Management, the small, five-person KM team was being overrun with publishing requests. The organization had recently adopted a new corporate governance program and was implementing several new software systems to address outstanding technical debt. These changes created a demand for new and updated knowledge that was far greater than the team could support.

As a precursor to the Distributed Publishing initiative, I’d led the KM team through several smaller initiatives aimed at standardizing and streamlining internal KM processes, and I’d hired four contractors to help reduce the backlog. Although these measures helped, it became clear that incremental fixes weren't enough, and that hiring additional help wasn't a viable long-term option. What we needed was a fundamentally different model for publishing knowledge.


## Challenge

The challenge in implementing a new model was both procedural and cultural. In many ways, the cultural challenges were harder to solve.

**Procedurally,** the publishing process had become a bottleneck. The standard workflow required SMEs to submit drafts of knowledge articles to the KM team, who would then review them for governance and compliance, lightly edit them for style and usability, and publish them to the knowledge base. When organizational change had occurred gradually, this was a reasonable model. But as demand accelerated, the publishing queue grew faster than the team could clear it: the backlog reached 300 requests, average TTP climbed to nearly 60 days, and compliance escalations — errors caused by outdated or inaccurate articles — had become a recurring problem.

**Culturally,** the challenge was more complex. Many teams at BECU operated in functional silos, and many were resistant to taking on work they considered someone else’s job. Asking SMEs to take on more responsibility for publishing, even with training and support, risked being perceived as KM offloading its duties onto others. Some SMEs, on the other hand, were eager to take on the responsibility for publishing, but they were largely unaware of the governance responsibilities inherent in the work. The KM team, too, expressed concern, particularly about whether governance would hold and whether the overall quality of the KB would degrade when publishing was no longer centralized. These were legitimate concerns that helped shape the fundamental design of the initiative.

Getting everyone to see the Distributed Publishing initiative as an opportunity rather than a burden would require trust and collaboration— not just a process change, but a shift in perception. 


## Approach

Although it was my responsibility, as the leader of KM, to solve this problem, I wanted to work through the solution collaboratively with those closest to the work. They were intimately familiar with the process and its pain points, and their buy-in would be essential for the initiative to be a success.

I initiated a series of collaborative workshops with a hypothesis grounded in my experience with Knowledge-Centered Service (KCS) and the benefits of empowering knowledge producers. The hypothesis was that enabling select, trained SMEs to publish directly to the KB without routing every request through KM would be key to breaking the publishing bottleneck. But I'd learned from experience that announcing a pre-defined solution from the start rarely works. Instead, I structured the workshops as discovery sessions framed by the Lean Six Sigma DMAIC model, working with leadership from three of BECU's core lending divisions (Mortgage, Loan Production, and Loan Servicing) to analyze the problem before converging on a solution. Together we:

  -	Analyzed the problem in detail
  -	Created, refined, and validated a succinct problem statement
  -	Brainstormed solution ideas 
  -	Collaborated on defining our best end-to-end solution
  -	Designed, tested, and implemented the solution

The workshops helped to focus the process design, encourage teamwork, and give stakeholders a sense of ownership in an initiative that they might have otherwise resisted.

Rather than focusing on just the publishing phase, we mapped the full knowledge lifecycle in detail, from knowledge creation through knowledge use. By mapping out each activity in the end-to-end process, we identified gaps, redundancies (waste), unnecessary handoffs, and potential points of failure that wouldn’t have been considered if we hadn’t examined the broader context. 

In the end, the solution emerged collaboratively, and the teams who’d be most affected by the change had helped design it.


## Solution

The solution was what came to be known as Distributed Publishing, a model that effectively inverted the traditional publishing workflow. Rather than routing all knowledge articles through the KM team, select SMEs would be empowered to publish directly to the knowledge base themselves, optionally calling upon KM for assistance whenever needed.

To be eligible to participate in Distributed Publishing, SMEs needed to be nominated by their managers and approved as recognized experts in their domain. Approved SMEs would then be certified through KM training that emphasized governance and quality controls. Once certified, these SMEs were designated as Advanced SMEs. 
Governance controls were integrated into the end-to-end process. Before publishing content to the knowledge base, articles passed through a tiered approval chain: peer review, supervisor approval, and, where warranted, compliance administrator sign-off. Advanced SMEs also worked from a structured checklist confirming conformance to templates, style guidelines, usability standards, and audience appropriateness, with a final check to ensure that the content wasn't duplicative or contradictory to existing knowledge.

To implement the initiative, I adopted a hybrid Agile/Waterfall approach. At the time, BECU was actively encouraging teams to work in a more agile fashion, and I felt that a lightweight Scrum approach would work well here. We tracked work on a LeanKit Planview Kanban board and maintained a parallel project schedule to manage milestones and dependencies.

Planning centered on three pillars: 
  -	**Stakeholder engagement:** Secured executive sponsorship and built support across the teams who would be most affected by the change. 
  -	**Change readiness:** Analyzed current and future states for potential points of friction, and developed the communications, training curriculum, and documentation that would be needed to help ease the transition. 
  -	**Deployment:** Designed a rollout cadence that would allow us to learn and adjust before scaling.

We estimated that we’d be able to release our first deployment in three months. This estimate included the time it’d take to update the software and the corresponding documentation, as well as the time it’d take to build out the training curriculum. 

We deployed Distributed Publishing in three consecutive waves (one for each stakeholder group), enabling approximately 15 Advanced SMEs per wave to author and publish knowledge directly to the knowledge base. The initial pilot launched in ten weeks — two weeks ahead of schedule — and was monitored closely for issues and corrections before we proceeded with the next two deployments.


## Results

The results of the Distributed Publishing initiative demonstrated a sustainable, repeatable process for publishing accurate, consistent, compliant knowledge in a timely manner. The collaboration with cross-functional stakeholders, particularly the inclusion of those who were skeptical about the initiative, ensured that we addressed identified risks in both our governance design and the Advanced SME training. 

**Key results:**
  -	Time-to-publish (TTP) decreased from up to 60 days to 7 days — an 88% reduction 
  -	Compliance escalations fell from 3 per week to 0
  -	The publishing backlog was reduced from 300 requests to a manageable ~25 
  -	SME satisfaction increased from 1.9 to 4.2 (out of 5.0)
  -	On-time publishing improved from 38% to 96% 
  -	The 4 contractors hired as a temporary measure were no longer needed, saving a considerable amount of money 
  -	3 permanent KM staff who previously supported publishing work were redeployed to focus on higher-value knowledge strategy activities 

Beyond the metrics, the initiative strengthened KM's relationship with the SME community, as they became more active participants in the knowledge-sharing mission rather than passive contributors to it.  

## Lessons Learned

Looking back on the initiative, a few lessons stand out: Several we’d do again, and a few we’d do differently.

**What we'd do differently:**
  -	**Listen to detractors sooner — and more personally.** This was a management error on my part. When a member of my own team raised concerns about the initiative, I encouraged her to voice them in the group setting and assured her they would be addressed — and they were. But I later realized that I should’ve set aside dedicated 1:1 time to listen to her concerns privately first, rather than implying that she should route the concerns through the group. That conversation would have shown her how much I valued her perspective. Ensuring that someone’s concerns will be addressed isn’t the same as making them feel heard. This was a reminder that influenced my future interactions with my team.
  -	**Communicate more broadly and more regularly.** We communicated well around key milestones and dependencies, but we probably should’ve sent more routine updates to sponsors and senior leadership. In retrospect, a brief weekly update, even when there was little to report, would have maintained visibility and reinforced confidence in the initiative's progress.
  -	**Train smaller groups.** Training 15 SMEs per training session was difficult, particularly when working on hands-on exercises in the authoring tool. In future training sessions, we limited the number of participants to five to ensure that we could give everyone the attention that they deserved.

**What we'd do again:**
  -	**Take the time to do it right.** The collaborative workshops were among the most valuable investments we made. Resisting the urge to move fast allowed the solution to form naturally. A prescriptive approach wouldn’t have worked nearly as well.
  -	**Lead with a hypothesis, not a detailed plan.** Initiating the discovery process with an open question rather than a predetermined answer — even when I had a strong hypothesis — enabled others to contribute openly and freely. The workshops became as much about team building as problem solving.
  -	**Frame the work in DMAIC.** The Lean Six Sigma framework forced us to understand the problem thoroughly before designing potential solutions. Without that discipline, I'm not sure that we would’ve identified the nuances that made the initiative successful.
  - **Embrace Agile.** Working within a lightweight Scrum framework increased transparency and kept everyone aligned. Taking a balanced approach was effective for a cross-functional initiative involving teams unfamiliar with Agile.
